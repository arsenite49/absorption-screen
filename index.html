📖 New to the stock market? Plain-English guide to this screen

The idea in one line: when big, patient investors quietly buy a stock over weeks (called accumulation), it leaves two fingerprints — the stock stops falling on days the whole market falls (their buying absorbs the selling), and more shares than usual get delivered, i.e. actually kept overnight instead of day-traded. This screen hunts for both fingerprints at once.

  • Bhavcopy — NSE's official end-of-day report: every stock's price, volume and delivery for one trading day. You load one file per day.
  • Benchmark / index — a stand-in for "the market as a whole", e.g. NIFTY Smallcap 250 (an average of 250 smaller companies). Every stock is judged against it.
  • Down-day — a day the benchmark fell more than your threshold. These are the only days that count here: anything can rise in a rising market, but refusing to fall in a falling one takes real, determined buyers.
  • Delivery — shares transferred into the buyer's account because they were held past market close. Day-traders' shares are never delivered, so unusually high delivery ≈ someone genuinely building a position, not churn.
  • Score — each surviving stock is rated 0–100 relative to the others in this run, across five signals. It ranks evidence of accumulation; it is not a buy recommendation.

Throughout the app, hover or tap any i mark for a plain-English explanation of the control or number next to it.

03 · Signal validation

Walk-forward test — does the score actually predict anything?

The honest test for any screen: pretend you ran it at several past dates using only data available then, then check the future you couldn't have seen. Stocks are grouped into deciles by score at each date (D1 = top 10%) and each decile's average excess forward return over the benchmark is measured. If the score works, D1 beats D10 and the bars step down roughly in order. i

🧭 How do I read this? — plain-English guide

What just happened: a time machine. The app rewound to several past dates, scored every stock using only what it could have known then, and then fast-forwarded to grade its own picks — like grading old predictions against what actually happened. No hindsight is involved, which is what makes this test honest.

  • Deciles — at each past date, the ranked stocks are split into 10 equal groups by score. D1 = the 10% the score liked most, D10 = the 10% it liked least.
  • Excess return — the grade is not "did the stock go up" but "did it beat the market". +1% excess means it did 1 point better than the benchmark, even if both fell.

The three checks, in order:

  • 1 · The staircase. A working score puts the tallest blue bar at D1, stepping down toward D10. Bars of similar height in no particular order = the score is shuffling stocks randomly. (Pure chance alone makes the ordering hold in ≈4–5 of the 9 adjacent pairs — so 4/9 means nothing, 8/9 means a lot.)
  • 2 · D1 hit rate. How often the top decile beat the benchmark. 50% is a coin flip; 55%+ is interesting; below 50% means the score's favourites lost more often than they won.
  • 3 · The D1−D10 spread. The single headline number: what you'd have earned per period by holding the score's favourites instead of its rejects. Clearly positive = the score separates winners from losers. Near zero = it doesn't, whatever the other numbers look like.

Common traps:

  • All bars red / negative — your whole filtered universe lagged the benchmark that period. That's a statement about the market regime (e.g. smallcaps underperforming the index), not by itself a failure of the ranking — the ranking's job is only to order stocks within the universe. Check the staircase and spread as usual.
  • Few evaluation dates — 8 dates in one 6-month window is one market regime, not a proof. A verdict only firms up across longer windows and different conditions.
  • The tuning trap — if you adjust weights until this chart looks beautiful, you have memorised the past, not predicted the future. Change weights for a reason, then validate — never the other way round.
  • Horizon mismatch — absorption tends to resolve over weeks. If 40 sessions shows nothing, try 10 and 20 before concluding the signal is dead (and vice versa).

Bottom line: green verdict = the score is a usable shortlist for further research. Flat or inverted verdict = keep using the screen to find candidates, but let your fundamental second gate do the deciding.

Method

Returns are computed as CLOSE ÷ PREV_CLOSE − 1 from the bhavcopy itself. NSE adjusts PREV_CLOSE on ex-dates, so splits, bonuses and dividends do not create phantom moves. Down-day RS is the average of (stock − benchmark) return across benchmark down-days, weighted by the size of the benchmark's fall — holding firm on a −1.5% day counts for more than on a −0.4% day. Downside beta is the OLS slope of stock return on benchmark return using down days only. Delivery surge compares median delivered quantity in the recent window against the stock's own prior baseline — never an absolute delivery-% threshold. Delivery asymmetry is average delivered quantity on the stock's up days versus its down days; accumulation concentrates delivery on up days. Stocks that hit price bands on more than 5% of sessions are excluded — circuit-driven "strength" is not absorption.

This tool ranks evidence of accumulation. It is a first-line quantitative sieve, not a recommendation. It cannot distinguish informed buying from operator or promoter positioning — fundamental screens remain the second gate. Educational use only; not investment advice; no buy/sell/hold calls.
